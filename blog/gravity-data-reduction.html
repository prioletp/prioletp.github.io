Data Reduction with the GRAVITY Pipeline

⚙️

VLTI/GRAVITY is a K-band (2.2 µm) beam combiner that provides spectrally dispersed visibilities, closure phases, and differential phases with exceptional sensitivity and precision. It is widely used for studying the Galactic Centre, AGN, young stellar objects, and — increasingly — exozodiacal dust. This tutorial covers the essential steps of reducing GRAVITY data, based on experience I gained during my visit at ESO Santiago.

Prerequisites

• The GRAVITY pipeline installed via ESO Reflex or EsoRex.
• Raw data from the ESO archive (science target + calibrator observations).
• Static calibration files (wavelength tables, bad pixel maps, etc.).
• A working Python installation for post-processing and plotting.

Step 1: Understanding the GRAVITY Data Structure

GRAVITY observations consist of several types of files: acquisition images, fringe tracker data, science channel (SC) interferometric data, and metrology files. The SC data are what you primarily reduce to obtain visibilities and phases. GRAVITY operates in two spectral resolutions: LOW (R ~ 22), MEDIUM (R ~ 500), and HIGH (R ~ 4000).

Step 2: Running the Pipeline

The key pipeline recipes are:

1. gravity_dark: Creates master dark frames from dark exposures.
2. gravity_p2vm: Computes the pixel-to-visibility matrix (P2VM) from internal calibration source data. This is a critical calibration step that maps detector pixels to interferometric observables.
3. gravity_vis: Extracts raw visibilities, closure phases, and differential phases from the science data using the P2VM.
4. gravity_viscal: Calibrates the raw observables using a calibrator star observation, producing the final calibrated OIFITS file.

Step 3: Calibration Strategy

Choosing a good calibrator is essential. The calibrator should be:

• Unresolved or with a well-known angular diameter.
• Close in sky position to the science target (ideally within a few degrees).
• Observed close in time to the science observation to minimise transfer function variations.
• Of similar brightness to avoid saturation or low-SNR issues.

Step 4: Post-Processing and Quality Checks

After running the pipeline, inspect the calibrated OIFITS files:

• Check the transfer function by plotting calibrator visibilities vs. time.
• Verify closure phases of calibrators are consistent with zero.
• Look for outlier baselines — sometimes one telescope has issues (e.g., AO problems) that degrade specific baselines.
• Use tools like OIFITSExplorer or Python libraries (astropy.io.fits, oimodeler) to visualise and compare your data.

Tips from Experience

• The fringe tracker (FT) data can be useful for diagnosing atmospheric conditions and fringe SNR — don't ignore it.
• For faint targets, pay close attention to the integration time and consider whether coherent or incoherent averaging is more appropriate.
• When combining GRAVITY data with MATISSE or PIONIER data for multi-wavelength studies, ensure consistent calibration approaches across instruments.
• The GRAVITY pipeline is well-documented — the ESO pipeline manual is your best reference.
